Why Social Roles Exist & Why We Can’t “Just Be Ourselves”

Gabriel Agyeman-Dua • Composition I • Mr. Tierney • 7 December 2025

By: Gabriel Agyeman-Dua

“Being yourself isn’t possible if society has already decided who you’re supposed to be.”

Target Audience Statement

My target audience is young adults between the ages of sixteen to twenty-two who believe identity is something they have full control over. This age range tends to repeat the phrase “just be yourself;” however, they don’t tend to acknowledge how difficult it is in reality. I chose this audience because people in this age group are beginning to form their identities while confronting new social pressures in work, school, etc. My overall message is that identity is not simply a personal choice, but something shaped by stereotypes & expectations outside one’s control. To persuade my intended audience, I used a relatable tone that reflects their experiences, while maintaining credibility through my analysis, evidence, and structure. The goal of my text is to assist my audience in understanding that authenticity is complicated, not because individuals are at fault, but because society restricts who you're allowed to be.

Introduction

When we grow up, we tend to hear the phrase that sounds individualistic: “just be yourself”. It’s repeated so often that it’s somewhat embedded into our social script, an instruction for authenticity that presumes identity is created from within. But as we begin to live longer, we confront this script with our own contracting experiences. People are shaped by the expectations placed on them & treated differently based on the categories people assume they belong in, and we’re pressured to behave in ways that fit our defined roles. If society truly wanted us to “be ourselves,” then why are there so many structures, judgments, & expectations placed on us that push us towards the roles that limit us? To develop an understanding of why people can’t simply exist outside of these roles, we can look at how three authors: Amy Tan, Brent Staples, & Gloria Anzaldúa describe these invisible forces that shape our identity. From these three authors, I’ll use their personal experiences to reveal that societal roles exist because they create order for those in power & make people easier to categorize(stereotyping). These roles can offer a rapport; however, more often than not, they restrict our individuality. Together, these stories will make this message clear: being yourself isn’t possible if society has already decided who you're supposed to be.

Amy Tan — Mother Tongue

Societal roles exist because they assist society in stereotyping individuals into specific categories; these categories aren’t neutral, either, they’re built on assumptions due to an individual's race, language, gender, and culture. In Amy Tan’s ‘’Mother Tongue,’’ we see that language can put people into roles before they can even break out of it. Tan elaborates on this, explaining how people treated her mother differently due to her incompetence when speaking English(‘’broken English’’); however, Tan was treated more seriously when she spoke on behalf of her mother. Society didn’t bother listening to her mother due to the role they presumptuously assigned to her, due to her limited English, the role of “limited English speaker” carries stereotypes of incompetence or a lack of intellectual capabilities. These roles don’t randomly emerge; they are formed because society prioritizes one dominant way of speaking and silences the others. In other words, these roles exist to maintain linguistic hierarchy and social order.

Brent Staples — Black Men in Public Spaces

Similarly, in Brent Staples’ ‘’Black Men in Public Spaces,’’ stereotypes begin to arise due to deep-rooted fears and racism. Staples describes how strangers treated him and assigned him the role of a dangerous Black man. Even though Staples described himself as a softy for simply walking through public spaces, he was assumed to be a dangerous individual. Staples’ notes how a woman once “They seem to set their faces on neutral and, with their purse straps strung across their chests bandolier style, they forge ahead as though bracing themselves against being tackled.”(Page 1, Paragraph 6) This woman didn’t even know Staples at all; however, due to Black men being historically viewed as vicious individuals capable of horrific acts of violence, she braced herself for what she assumed could happen rather than dropping her stereotypical beliefs, and this overpowers Staples' actual identity. This role exists due to society historically assigning the label of violent individuals capable of vicious acts to Black men, forming public perception.

Gloria Anzaldúa — Culture & Identity

In Gloria Azaldúa’s “How to Tame a Wild Tongue,” roles come from cultural and institutional pressure. Azaldúa elucidates on how Chicana women face the expectation of being able to speak “proper” English & behave acceptably, and suppress their culture to appear professional. Azaldúa writes that she was disciplined in school for speaking Spanish, and her accent was “incorrect.” This shows how institutions created and reinforced role assimilation. The role of a professional American student is to erase differences, and not to celebrate them. Institutions defend these roles because they reinforce the dominant culture and keep people under control.

Conclusion

Together, Tan, Azaldúa, and Staples' stories explain how socially defined roles don’t appear naturally. They exist because they benefit dominant groups, simplify social interactions, and maintain long-standing systems of power. Although social roles may often restrict people's individuality, they can sometimes offer identity or belonging. Tan acknowledges that speaking multiple versions of English provided her access to different communities. Tan wrote that her husband's English felt intimate. “It has become our language of intimacy, a different sort of English that relates to family talk”(Page 1, Paragraph 4), suggesting that a linguistic role created an intimate environment rather than distance. For Tan, moving between roles assisted her in understanding her spouse better and eventually empowered her to become a writer. This provides evidence that not all social roles are, in fact, harmful. Azaldúa also recognizes the value of shared cultural identity. Azaldúa goes on to explain how Chicano Spanish helped her connect to her family & history. Even though society attempted to shame her for speaking it, her linguistic role as a Chicana woman allowed her to preserve her culture. The good in this role comes from connectedness with her family, knowing that she isn’t alone in her struggle. In Staples’ case, the positive side of roles is rather complicated. Staples mentions how he started whistling popular melodies from Beethoven & Vivaldi during his leisurely night walks so that people felt less threatened by him(Staples). While this adaptation stems from unfair stereotypes, it assists Staples in navigating the world with an increased amount of security. In a twisted way, understanding the role others placed upon him helped him in avoiding dangerous misunderstandings. The benefit is survival, not acceptance. So while social roles may provide some benefit to identity & community, they’re rarely humanly designed. The “good” they offer tends to be accidental, while the harm they cause is more deliberate. Society claims to value individualism, but real life contradicts this idea. People can’t simply “just be themselves” because society judges them through expectations, and these expectations limit how we conduct ourselves, speak, and even how we operate in public spaces. In Tan’s experience, “being herself” meant speaking multiple English variations, but society punished her and her mother for it. Tan writes that she had to switch between “perfect English” in professional settings because her authentic voice wasn’t valued in those spaces. Tan’s authentic self wasn’t respected, only the version of herself that fit with society's standards. For Staples, being himself an educated & thoughtful man didn’t matter much. Society still viewed him as a dangerous Black man. His identity contradicted stereotypes that he never agreed to. Even though Staples did nothing wrong, he had to constantly adapt to situations to avoid conflict. Being himself wasn’t an option; safety forced him to perform a role that calmed other people's fears. For Azaldúa, “just being herself” meant embracing her mixed language and culture; however, schools, workplaces, and her community pressured her into changing. Azaldúa wrote, “Wild tongues can't be tamed, they can only be cut out,”(Page 2, Paragraph 2), meaning that society demanded assimilation and disciplined those who resisted. Authenticity becomes a rebellion, not a natural state. All three authors show that identity isn’t a personal choice; it’s shaped and restricted by structures that existed before we’re even born. Beyond individual stories, academic research also shows that socially defined roles operate as invisible rules that guide behavior. In the scholarly article “Organizational Citizenship Behavior: What’s Gender Got to Do With It?” Waismel-Manor and colleagues explain that society encourages people to conform to expectations based on their gender, culture, & perceived identity, even when those expectations may limit who you are as a person. They argue that people are often rewarded for conducting themselves in a way that aligns with stereotypes and are penalized when they violate them, which mirrors the experiences of Tan, Staples, & Azaldúa (Waismel-Manor et al.). This research is supportive of the idea that social roles don’t simply appear; they’re enforced through institutions, workplaces, and day-to-day interactions, making it difficult for individuals to freely express themselves authentically. Socially defined roles exist because society depends on them to keep the population in order and simplify human complexity. These roles can sometimes create a connected, flourishing community or protect people from misunderstandings, but they often have limitations when it comes to individuality and silence the parts of our identity that don’t conform to these expectations. Tan reveals how language determines whose words are deemed valuable and get listened to. Staples reveals how race dictates people's perception and treatment of you. Anzaldúa exposes how cultural and institutional pressure strips people of their own authentic selves. Even the research shows that these expectations aren’t random; systems reward following stereotypes and punish them when they step outside of the roles that society assigns, which makes genuine self-expression even more difficult. Together, they prove that telling someone to “just be yourself” ignores the realities of identity in a world where roles are reinforced through institutions, power, and predetermined stereotypes from history. Authenticity is not a simple act; it’s a negotiation with forces larger than us. Understanding these forces is the first step towards challenging them, and toward creating a world where being authentic is actually possible.
